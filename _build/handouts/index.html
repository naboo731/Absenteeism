The Scenario

There is a summit of managers who are meeting to discuss employee happiness and satisfaction. The managers work for a variety of companies in various industries. One of the panels will discuss worker absenteeism in order to understand how to craft a more modern, flexible Paid Time Off policy for the future of work.

As a data analyst who works for one of the managers on the absenteeism panel, you have been tasked with conducting an analysis on large available dataset of absenteeism to draw out insights that might be helpful for the panel discussion.

Avoiding Data Traps

Hyman’s Maxim: Don’t try to explain HOW something works until you have confirmed that it does, in fact, work.

In order to be sure you are building your analysis on a solid foundation, you need to check your data for quality. You can do this by looking at the following four types of Null Hypotheses and confirming that they are not present in your dataset.

  1. Correlation Null Hypothesis: Two different traits, such as whether someone is a social drinker and whether they are a social smoker, are not actually different. This should not be the case because statistically, there must be at least a few people who are ONLY social smokers or ONLY social drinkers, or NEITHER.

  2. Independent Samples Null Hypothesis: Two different populations (such as people younger than 30 and older than 40) actually behave the same. This should not be the case because different populations should have different habits.

  3. Chi-squared independence Null Hypothesis: Two things that ARE definitely related don’t seem to have a relationship in the data. For example, distance from work and transportation expense most certainly should have a relationship.

  4. Repeated measures Null Hypothesis: Depite changing conditions or time, the measure or behavior does not change. For example, tracking a single ID would give you the all absences for a single employee. With a repeated measure for an employee, we would expect values for “reason for absence”, “average workload”, etc to change with time.

Choosing Relationships to Visualize

In this section, you’ll explore relationships within the Absenteeism datset.

  1. First, upload your TSV to Google Sheets.

  2. Next, setup Conditional Formatting to provide meaningful coloring to the various values in your dataset. You will need to consider the range (min, max) for each column and set up conditional formatting to be appropriate for each set of values. For example, a column that is Boolean (either 0 or 1), you might want to use red/green to mean 0/1 respectively. For a column with values ranging from 1-100, you might use dark green for 90-100, light green for 80-90, etc.
